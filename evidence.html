01 / Historical Anomalies

The Myth of Dependable Deterrence

The architecture of global security is built upon the assumption of Rational Deterrence — the idea that systems are managed by perfect actors with perfect data. However, a pattern of near misses with nuclear war suggests that perfection is an illusion sometimes enabled by luck. The following are just three examples of the many close calls with nuclear wars we have managed to escape in the past.

Case Study: The 1983 Petrov Incident

Protocol demanded an immediate retaliatory strike, but duty-officer Stanislav Petrov disregarded orders and delayed reporting the system's multiple warnings. Later investigation revealed that an early-warning satellite had mistaken sunlight reflecting off clouds as incoming missiles. But what if an officer who followed procedure had been on duty that night? Soviet ICBMs would have flown, and WWIII would have followed.

Able Archer 83

At the height of the Cold War, the Soviet Union nearly launched a nuclear strike against the United States and Western Europe. When a NATO military exercise called Able Archer was mistaken for the start of an imminent invasion, Soviet nuclear forces went on high alert. Nuclear artillery was moved to frontline positions for the first time. Ballistic missile submarines and strategic bombers prepared for war. Once again, our civilization may still exist because an American officer ignored official protocol and acted upon his instinct instead.

The 1995 Black Brant Scare

Russia's presidential nuclear command suitcase was activated for the first time in history when radar operators mistook a Norwegian weather rocket as an American ICBM coming toward Moscow. Russian President Boris Yeltsin immediately prepared his mobile nuclear command center for retaliation. Two minutes before "impact" were all that remained for Yeltsin to decide the fate of the world when a false alarm was declared.

02 / Atmospheric Modeling

The Mechanics of Global Famine

Our Planet Project Foundation utilizes advanced 3D aerosol modeling to project the impact of soot injection into the stratosphere. Previous models underestimated the "self-lofting" effect, where black carbon absorbs sunlight, heats the surrounding air, and rises into the upper atmosphere where it cannot be washed away by rain.

Our findings indicate that a major exchange between America and Russia would inject an estimated 150 million tons of soot into the stratosphere, reducing incoming sunlight by approximately 70%. This would lower global temperatures by an average of 10°C for nearly a decade, effectively eliminating growing seasons worldwide and ending global food production.
